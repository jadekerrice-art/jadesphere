2075: A New Era of Intelligence, Energy, and Work

A forward-looking essay on AI integration, energy infrastructure, and labor market disruption.

By Jade Adamson • Published 2026 • 8 min read

Introduction

The world today is virtually unrecognizable compared to 1975. Over the last five decades, technology accelerated through personal computers, smartphones, and artificial intelligence, reshaping society through connectivity, automation, and instant access to information.

Less than 1 percent of the world’s information was stored digitally in 1975. By 2012, about 99 percent was stored in digital form (Hilbert, 2020). Digital technologies also spread rapidly, reaching around half of the developing world’s population in only two decades (United Nations, n.d.).

Background and History of Technological Evolution

The world’s capacity to communicate and store information expanded by roughly 25 to 35 percent per year across recent decades, doubling every 2.5 to 3 years (Hilbert, 2020). This set the base for the digital economy and new social patterns tied to online communication.

In 1995, about 0.7 percent of the world used the internet. By 2025, global usage reached 68 percent (Per Scholas, 2025). The benefits touch healthcare and education through early diagnostics, tracking, and remote learning access (United Nations, n.d.).

The cost is real. Digital infrastructure relies on data centers and compute-heavy systems. Computational capacity has grown at nearly 80 percent per year, outpacing communication or storage growth (Hilbert, 2020). Major tech companies have also reported emissions increases alongside data center expansion (Carbon Brief, 2025).

Issues and Various Points of View

AI capability and inequality

Supporters highlight AI’s potential in diagnostics, cybersecurity, education, and research (Stanford HAI, 2025). Critics emphasize uneven access, algorithm bias, opaque decision-making, and privacy erosion (Stanford HAI, 2025). A balanced view supports stronger regulation, transparency, and broad digital literacy (Deloitte, 2025).

Energy strain and sustainability

AI can improve forecasting, grid optimization, and climate modeling (Deloitte, 2025). At the same time, data centers and training can pressure electricity and water demand (IEA, 2025). Sustainable scaling requires efficiency improvements and transparent reporting (IEA, 2025).

Work disruption

Historically, technology has created more jobs than it destroyed. A large share of workers are now in roles that did not exist decades ago (Goldman Sachs, 2025). Still, AI can replicate complex cognitive work. The World Economic Forum reports growth in tech roles but also risk to clerical and service work (WEF, 2025).

Results of Interviews and Observations

Informal interviews across Generation Alpha, Millennials, and Baby Boomers show a pattern: younger participants normalize automation, Millennials expect innovation, and Boomers focus on sustainability, health, and infrastructure strain.

Summary

The next fifty years will be shaped by AI, modern energy networks, and changing labor dynamics. The question is not whether AI changes society. The question is how we navigate those changes with governance, sustainable infrastructure, reskilling, and equal access.
